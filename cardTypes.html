Card Types

This is some filler text and should be replaced with something that introduces the topic and gets the reader interested in learning more.

Land

Lands are the most important part of a deck. Lands give you the mana you need to play the rest of your cards. You can only play up to 1 land card on each of your turns during either of your main phases.

Basic Lands

The most common type of card is the “basic land”. These are simple cards that can be tapped for a single mana of their color:

  • "Plains" = White
  • "island" = Blue
  • "Swamp" = Black
  • "Mountain" = Red
  • "Forest" = Green

Creature

Creatures are your main line of defense. They are your attackers and defenders. Each creature has a “Power” and “Toughness”. This is always written as “P/T” with the power first, before the slash, and the toughness after. This is the same when an effect gives a creature “+1/+1”, or less commonly “+0/+1”.

Instant

Instants are cards that you can play anytime you have “Priority”. Without going into specifics, basically you can play these in response to other actions, even if it’s not your turn. This is what is known as “Instant speed”, where you can play a card out of turn. While only instants can be played at instant speed by default, other effects such as “flash” can allow you to play other cards out of turn.

Sorcery

Sorceries are similar to instants, but can only be played during the main phases of your turn. This is known as “Sorcery speed”. Simply put, anything that isn’t “instant speed” is “sorcery speed” and thus can only be played at certain times.

Sorceries are one-time effects, where the card goes to the graveyard after it resolves. They also tend to be more powerful than instants and tend to cost more mana as well.

Enchantment

These cards give an effect to something else. Typically either a benefit (buff) or a drawback (de-buff).

Auras are a specific type of enchantment that you place on a player and affect either them or all of the cards they control. Non-Aura enchantments will have you target something specific such as a creature.

Artifact

Depending on the card, artifacts are kind of like a mix between creatures and enchantments. They can give general effects like an enchantment would, or they might have specific abilities for you to use.

Some artifacts, known as equipment, are attached to creatures in the same way you would cast an enchantment. There are two big factors that distinguish equipment:

  1. You play the artifact first, then you must also pay the “equip” cost to attach it to the creature. This can be done right after casting it, though it’s not required.
  2. Similarly, you can re-equip your equipment, or attach it to a different creature by paying the “equip” cost.
  3. Equipment doesn’t leave the battlefield when the creature they’re attached to dies. On the other hand, enchantments leave the battlefield (into the graveyard) if they aren’t either an aura or attached to something.

Battle

Battles are a special type of card that you play on an opponent. That opponent is then in charge of protecting it. You can either target it with a spell or ability or attack it in the same way you would a player or planeswalker.

Each battle has an immediate effect, like playing a sorcery, and a second side to the card. Once the battle is defeated, the player who finished it off (did the last damage) then gets to cast the other side (there’s no mana cost). The backside will have any of the other card types on it meaning that the reward could be a powerful creature or an artifact with a unique ability. It could also be a one-time effect such as a sorcery or instant.

Planeswalker

Planeswalkers are unique and powerful cards. Each planeswalker has a number in the bottom-right (where you find the power/toughness on a creature) which represents how many “loyalty counters” it enters the battlefield with. “Loyalty counters” are like its health, once the planeswalker loses all of its loyalty counters, it is defeated (leaves the battlefield).

Loyalty Abilities

You will find at least one “Loyalty ability” (typically more than one) on each planeswalker. On your turn (sorcery speed), you can activate one of each planeswalker’s loyalty abilities. Each ability will come with a “cost” in loyalty counters. Usually a planeswalker will have a “plus ability”, that adds loyalty counters to it, and one or two “minus abilities”, that will remove that many loyalty counters.

*Note that while creatures can have negative toughness, planeswalkers can not have negative loyalty counters. That means that you must have at least enough loyalty counters to activate the ability. For example, you can’t activate a loyalty ability with a cost of “-8” if the planeswalker only has 3 loyalty counters.
